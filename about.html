人肉搜索

Human Flesh Search Engine

Origin

人肉搜索 (rénròu sōusuǒ) — literally "human flesh search engine." Distributed, crowdsourced investigation where thousands of anonymous participants collectively identify, locate, and document targets using nothing but publicly available information and collective attention.

The practice emerged from Chinese internet culture in the early 2000s. The first documented case originated on Mop.com in 2001. By 2006 it had become a recognized social phenomenon with real consequences: people lost jobs, fled cities, were driven to suicide. The Chinese internet learned that distributed human attention, focused on a single target, is more powerful than any search algorithm.

The mechanism is simple. Someone posts a target. Thousands of people each contribute one small piece: a screenshot, a cross-referenced username, a location from a photo background, a workplace identified from a uniform. No single person does the work. The crowd does.

Western platforms developed their own versions. Doxbin. Kiwi Farms. The methods are the same. The framing is different. The capability is identical.

Notable Cases

The Kitten Killer of Hangzhou
A woman filmed herself crushing a kitten with her high heels. Within days, the crowd had identified her name, employer, phone number, and address from a reflection in the video. She was terminated from her government position.
2006, Hangzhou
The Bronze Mustache Incident
A tourist posted photos sitting on a bronze statue at a memorial site. Internet users identified him within 3 hours from his phone model, clothing brand, and a partial bus ticket visible in one image.
2008, Nanjing
Vancouver Riot Identification
After the 2011 Stanley Cup riot, crowdsourced identification from social media photos and video led to over 300 people being identified by name. Several faced criminal charges based solely on crowd-submitted evidence.
2011, Vancouver
Boston Marathon Bombing
Reddit users attempted to identify the bombers from crowd photos. They identified the wrong person. Sunil Tripathi, wrongly accused, was found dead. The crowd is powerful. The crowd is not always right.
2013, Boston
Hong Kong Protests
Both sides used 起底 (doxxing) extensively. Protesters identified undercover police. Pro-Beijing groups identified protesters, their families, and employers. The Hong Kong government passed anti-doxxing legislation in 2021 in direct response.
2019-2020, Hong Kong

What FLESHENGINE Does

FLESHENGINE structures what already happens. People already investigate each other online. The tools already exist. We provide the database, the taxonomy, and the aggregation layer.

Submit a target. The system pulls publicly available data and maps behavioral patterns, communication style, network position, and influence vulnerability across 12 vectors. Then the crowd fills in what automation misses: context, relationships, pattern shifts that only a human observer would notice.

Every fragment is timestamped, categorized, and weighted. The composite profile improves with each contribution. What was scattered across forum threads and group chats becomes structured, searchable intelligence.
